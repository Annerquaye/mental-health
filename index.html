Black youth mental health: A crisis of understanding of its causes needs swift resolution

By Richard Annerquaye Abbey

August 2022

Remote learning, one of the unmistaken legacies of the pandemic, was intended to create a healthy way for students to continue to learn and connect with their teachers. For Jasmine, 16, taking remote classes was a matter of logging on to zoom and turning off her mic and camera.

She took her lessons passively, resting her back against her bed’s headboard rather than at her study desk that came with an ergonomic chair. She felt comfortable. But in her comfort, she found taking lessons with undivided focus laborious.

Jasmine’s companion in those moments was her phone. She would occasionally text friends or check in on them on social media. Doing that allowed her to momentarily snap out of her boredom.

But what started as her inability to remain attentive in class turned into an academic struggle as she fell farther and farther behind. The lessons became difficult to follow. She would often not turn in assignments. When she did, she missed deadlines.

Jasmine knew she was hurting deep inside. She knew she had the intellectual ability to do her school work but also knew that she was now way too far behind to catch up. She felt like she was drowning and wanted help just to stay afloat.

In the past, when lessons became hard to follow, her peers came to her rescue before things could get out of hand. The virtual classes meant her support system was not readily available. That just added to her stress.

The 16-year-old had grown completely disillusioned and no longer wanted anything to do with school. She said she was not suicidal. Yet, occasionally, she imagined what the world without her would be like.

Despite her inner turmoil, she maintained a façade of smiles and laughter. Nevertheless, every single class piled on her worries. When she finally caught her parents’ attention, it wasn’t so much the plummeting grades (she never showed her parents) but her weight loss.

Jasmine has always been a picky eater as a child. When she turned 15, the 11th grader stood just under 5 feet and barely 100 pounds, giving her a slender stature. She never noticed her unexpected weight loss as her diet remained relatively the same. Her mom has always lamented that she eats too little food for someone her age. Jasmine would often shrug off those concerns but this time, her parents, startled by the weight loss, wanted a solution. Her mother took her to her pediatrician. Jasmine had noticed the change in her looks, too. Some of her dresses did not fit as snugly as they used. They hung loosely.

Jasmine’s pediatrician had known her since she was a baby. After several visits, the doctor could not find anything medically wrong but prescribed vitamin supplements to increase her appetite. Her skinny physique was also beginning to weigh on her, especially as her doctor could not point out what was wrong. She thought a diagnosis would ease her rising anxiety and provide her with a route to recovery.

The pediatrician checked in with Jasmine and her mother and was confident the vitamins would help her appetite. Jasmine wasn’t so sure. Still, she knew her parents were worried so figured she’d stick to them religiously.

Jasmine’s concerns about her health and academic issues had her mind racing in all directions. Her sleep was patchy. She yearned to understand what was happening to her body. She found no answers, only more worries which exacerbated her academic struggles.

The eleventh grader found short-lived comfort whenever she talked to her doctor, but she yearned for a trained ear, a therapist to help her makes sense of all that’s happened and tell her she was doing fine. She knew her problems extended deeper than vitamins and food.

One evening after her parents had finished dinner, she approached them. She told them she needed to speak to someone to help her with her emotional issues. Her parents were astounded. Sure, she wasn’t eating. And of course, she was bored with online school. Who wasn’t? But depressed? And needing a mental health expert? “They said, you can talk to us, and I was like ‘no!’, Jasmine recounts. “I would rather talk to an outside person.

Her father, Robert, 45, is a leader in the local Presbyterian Church and insisted she open up to him instead. After all, they are her parents, and they have the right to know whatever struggles she might have. “They lack understanding, thinking that they are in a better position to handle my stress.”

Her parents’ response to her therapist request confirmed her fears. She found her parents judgmental and believed only a professional therapist would understand her.

“If you cannot talk to me about it,” she recollects her dad saying, “there are other people in the family you could talk to.” That response, she says, set back the trust in the relationship she shared with her dad.

Jasmine’s parents, staunch Christians, migrated to the U.S. more than two decades ago from Ghana. Her father is a deacon in the Presbyterian Church of Ghana in Harlem, New York, with more than 200 congregants.

As a deacon, Robert is a member of the committee that sees to the running of the church. He is one of the music directors in the church’s choir, a position which further made him one of the known faces within the congregation. Mavis Tetteh, Jasmine’s mother, plays an active role in church too, helping run the Women’s Fellowship, one of the groups geared for women above age 40.

Even before the pandemic struck, concerns were mounting about a growing mental health crisis among Black people, especially their youth. Reports of depression, anxiety, and children attempting suicide were rampant. Then Covid-19 hit, intensifying fears that the infectious disease pandemic would ramp up the other, less easily noticeable, epidemic.

Resolving the crisis would require a cocktail of solutions that extended beyond the individual patient. Experts pointed to the lack of access to mental health care, its skyrocketing costs, and the sheer isolation and systemic racism that too many young Black people feel. To tackle what many see as an impossible and complex issue, last February, the New York Senate established a task force to explore ways to improve mental health and prevent suicide in Black residents between the ages of 5 to 18 years.

The task force was a response to mounting data. Experts found the virus disproportionately impacted Black people and increased pressure on legislators to act. One North Carolina study, for instance, found that while Black people made up about 20 percent of the population, they represented upwards of 35 percent of Covid cases there. Other studies echoed the findings. On top of the physical health crisis, the Black community was suffering emotionally.

The senate’s task force was also influenced by a Centers for Disease Control study by researchers at New York University’s McSilver Institute for Poverty Policy and Research. They gleaned results from a national school survey spanning 1991 to 2017 and found that while there has been an overall decline or no change at all among suicides, there was an increased rate of suicide attempts among black teens. Shockingly, black adolescents attempting to take their own lives rose by 73 percent.

The study, which analyzed the behaviors of 200,0000 adolescents, also found something strange: Self-reported suicide ideation, a key risk factor in suicide, was declining, yet more people attempted suicide than before. This seeming paradox means that more people are good at masking their suicidal thoughts yet just as driven to harm themselves. Generally, suicide ideation provides early warning signals about suicide which may tell where prevention efforts are needed. However, the study showed that these signals were hidden, a situation that may thwart suicide prevention programs.

New York University investigators, based on their findings, demanded a further probe into the rise in suicide and suicide attempts among Black adolescents. Time will tell whether the probe and task force is lip service or a step forward to save the children. The seven-member task force has a two-year mandate to explore ways to improve mental health and suicide prevention in a state where one in five African Americans is between ages 10 and 24, according to the latest American Community Survey (2016-2020) and CDC data (2010-2019) shows that suicide has remained a leading cause of deaths among adolescents.

While there are several underlying factors behind rising suicide rates among Black youth, the stigma surrounding mental health and barriers Black people face in accessing treatment are commonly cited.

However, an analysis of the locations of the New York State Office of Mental Health facilities providing services to adolescents shows that they are typically located within a 30-minute drive of where African Americans or Blacks live.

In fact, these facilities, which run various programs mental health programs, are within a 30-minute drive of almost everyone, irrespective of race, throughout the state. This finding would perplex those who believe these facilities are often sited in locations that disadvantage specifically African Americans, This analysis arrived at this conclusion by mapping the physical locations of facilities in the state alongside the racial distribution of the population to determine whether it will take anyone driving from their home to the nearest facility more than 30 minutes.

Some of these facilities also provide telehealth services which further enhance their reach. While this analysis showed an extensive geographic coverage of adolescent mental health services, determining whether these facilities are within a 15-minute drive would probably yield different results.

That notwithstanding, there appears to be a disproportionate number of Black kids seeking treatment for several mental health illnesses, data from the New York State Office of Mental Health has shown. In 2019, census data revealed that one in five youth (under age 20) were African American. Yet, one-third of the state’s under 20 population was Black youth using Medicaid to access intensive behavioral health services for children, meaning a more significant proportion of the Black kids' population is seeking mental health-related services.

Comparatively, despite making up 70 percent of the state’s under-20 population, white kids made up 40 percent of all kids who used Medicaid to access children's intensive behavioral services. Either they weren’t seeking help or had the money to pay for care.

There are limits to the data: While there is a noticeable difference in the utilization of children's mental health care services by race, it is worth noting that the Office of Mental Health data did not distinguish between race and ethnicity. For instance, the census treats Hispanics as an ethnicity while the state mental health office treats it as a race.

Mental health and adversity

As attention turns to the unfolding mental health crisis among African American populations, researchers are keen to unravel the mystery. Finding a lasting solution to the Black mental health crisis would first require drilling into the factors at the heart of the issue.

Myrna Weissman, who has spent the last 50 years teaching psychiatric epidemiology, which probes the causes and consequences of psychiatric disorders, said the spike in African American suicide could be explained by factors such as lack of trust in the health system as well as adversity. Since Black African Americans are disproportionately poor, they are mostly unable to afford mental healthcare, or it is not a priority. She also adds that because historically, Black patients believe they have not been treated fairly by the system, there is a lack of trust among black African Americans in the healthcare system.

Two years ago, Nakiya Melanie, a 23-year-old African American living in Brooklyn who has often battled depression and anxiety, could not get out of bed. The previous night she had celebrated her birthday with friends at her home.

It is almost routine for Melanie to have her friends come over for her birthday every year. It meant a lot to her. The seven days that followed her birthday night, she was stuck in bed with her depression and anxiety so intense like she had never experienced before. And when she decided with her boyfriend to seek medical help, the cost was one of the things they were very mindful of, in addition to getting a facility that was not too far from their home.

“I didn't have any information on what this entails, and no one was giving me any insight,” Melanie recollects. “Even though I knew I needed help, I can't do that (sign up without considering the cost). The stress of that money will not help the situation either.”

It took the couple nearly ten hours to find the ‘perfect’ facility at New York University. And when the two arrived at the hospital, Melanie was immediately rushed to the emergency ward of the psychiatric unit. Doctors diagnosed her with bipolar disorder. Dealing with severe depression and anxiety is not new to her, but what she dealt with after her birthday felt different. “People are likely to pay attention once you get suicidal,” says Melanie. “But the thing is, there’s always is a buildup to that part. Unless they educate themselves, people don't understand your actions when you have a mental illness.”

Calibrating suicide detection tools

Donna Barnes, who teaches at the Howard University Department of Psychiatry, argues that the instruments used in detecting suicide tendencies are not accurate in their prediction of suicide. “Many of the measurements or tools used do not work for us [African Americans] because there have not been enough studies [due to lack of funds] to prove our procedures are evidence-based,” she says.

Barnes believes that the National Institute of Mental Health, the federal agency that funds the bulk of research on mental disorders, would have to fund studies to help unravel the crisis unfolding. To do that, she suggested one of the means would be to commit funds to research into mental disorders being undertaken by Historically Black Colleges and Universities, also known as HBCUs.

In the last 30 years, only 20 of the listed 104HBCUs got funding to start their research. The bulk of the funding dollars, about 99 percent, have gone to traditional universities or institutions. Of course, it is possible that research into issues confronting the Black community, including mental health issues, are undertaken at other institutions. To fully flesh out these findings, further data into the kinds of studies, such as whether HBUC grants to study mental health, had been rejected.

The US Department of Education has identified 107 colleges in the United States as Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs). Of those 107, three are currently closed.

Between 1991 and 2021, about 20 of these got funding for research into mental disorders.

Over all, despite the reported crisis in mental health care, research funding in the last four years is somewhat declining.

That said, Barnes, teaches her students to always listen to their Black patients more, where possible, rather than rely solely on available literature to diagnose mental disorders. “I'm always telling my students that, when you have a 12-year-old Black male in front of you, and you are 30 years and white, don't try to reference what they're saying because you can't. You have no references to what he is saying to you. You can't even relate, so you pull out your evidence-based tools for your white population, which doesn't work for them.”

It is not strange to see Black kids expressing depression through anger, which Barnes says is not in sync with the 'dominant culture.' “Our evidence is different because there are certain risk factors and characteristics that we have that don’t match the dominant culture, and it’s not picked up,” she said.

Image: Rutgers University

Research conducted at Rutgers University, New Jersey, five years ago demonstrates how symptoms of mental health illness among Blacks could be misdiagnosed. In 2018, researchers examining medical records of about 1,700 patients, of all ages, concluded African Americans with severe depression are more likely to be misdiagnosed with schizophrenia established than any other racial group.

Even before Barnes, there have been concerns clinicians are prone to misdiagnosing certain mental health illnesses among Blacks mainly due to somewhat cultural differences in symptoms. That perception fired the curiosity of the Rutgers investigators. Their quest to find answers led them to probe whether clinicians accurately diagnosed about 600 Blacks and close to 1,100 with schizophrenia, a mental disorder characterized by hallucinations and delusions.

The findings were resounding. The Rutgers researchers found ample evidence that clinicians failed to account for mood symptoms when diagnosing schizophrenia among Blacks, adding that ‘racial bias,’ whether conscious or subconscious, played a role in diagnosing schizophrenia in African Americans.

“Why this phenomenon occurs remains unknown,” the authors said. “But it may represent either conscious or unconscious bias toward the meaning of psychiatric symptoms of African Americans or the effects on the clinical presentation of being a patient from a racial-ethnic minority group (for example, mistrust leading to less than full disclosure of presenting problems).”

Nevertheless, Dr. Lashonda Green, an African American psychologist, insists there’s a seeming lethargy among African Americans regarding mental health. “It's just that many Black people don't like to read [about mental health illness] and don't like to go to therapy,” she said. “A lot of black people don't like to volunteer to participate in psychological research or any other research.”

In her opinion and direct contrast to Barnes, the survey instruments used in screening for mental health illnesses are pretty sound and scientific, backed by empirical research. “There's no perfect instrument,” Dr. Green said. “I think people are looking for specific instruments for all types of races. Everyone's looking for something specialized and unique. But what is available is heavily empirically supported, and it does apply to Black people, but they are not presenting themselves.”

More trust issues

Two years ago, Helena Adu, 58, an African American Bronx resident, was unsettled by her 21-year-old daughter's fixation with issues surrounding failed relationships.

“Why do men cheat,” her daughter would often ask her, begging for answers. Adu’s sister was going through a divorce, making her think her daughter’s sudden curiosity about romantic relationships could be a result. But her daughter would not stop probing for answers on why such relationships break down.

Adu, a registered nurse in the Bronx, knew something was amiss when her daughter complained of being ‘sick’ and wanted to see a pastor rather than a physician. When she checked her daughter’s vital signs, everything seemed fine, but she still wanted to go to the hospital for an exhaustive assessment.

In the routine assessment, her daughter insisted on being married with four kids. The issue is that Adu had lived with her daughter for the last ten years and was not aware of any marriage not to talk about having kids. And that was when Adu’s suspicions were confirmed. Her daughter was referred to the psychiatric unit of the BronxCare Hospital for further assessment.

Later, Adu made another shocking discovery. Before her daughter's admission, she had texted several friends and relatives, accusing her immediate family of wanting to kill her. “Indeed, I didn’t know about those messages,” said Adu. “Those who received them were alarmed, but as a matter of fact, I did not want to see those messages. I didn’t want to be stressed by the content of these messages.”

Adu’s daughter had a long-distance romantic relationship with a boy from Ghana. The two had not seen each other in nearly ten years when her daughter left Ghana to reunite with her. The relationship blossomed until her daughter started receiving reports of her boyfriend’s infidelity. “She didn’t quite believe. But the evidence from her cousins would not stop coming,” said Adu. “It seems like she snapped when she could not bear it any longer.”

Her daughter was admitted and later diagnosed with bipolar disorder and put on medication, although she would later disagree with the diagnosis. A few weeks into her medication after her discharge, Adu’s daughter was also diagnosed with Schatzki’s ring, a medical condition that narrows the esophagus and makes it difficult to swallow. It took a minor surgical procedure, balloon dilation, to correct the condition, which Adu strongly believes was a side effect of her daughter’s medication, Abilify, although that was never confirmed. Subsequently, her doctors recommended a lower dosage and eventually took her off the drug.

Despite improvement in her daughter’s condition, there’s a part of Adu that still holds the view that her daughter was misdiagnosed. Though she concedes she’s not in a position to clinically diagnosed her daughter, she firmly believes the slew of questions asked her before making their decision were “all over the place” and could not have provided enough justification for a bipolar disorder diagnosis. “Sometimes, I believe they just needed to diagnose my daughter so they can just put her on a medication,” said Adu. “Perhaps, she was just depressed.”

While Adu may not have any evidence about the misdiagnosis, Barnes believes they are not out of place. She also accuses doctors treating mental health illnesses of being quick to prescribe medications, which may not always be the solution. “The first thing they pull out is a medication that doesn’t always work,” she said. “Many people take their own lives and don't have a mental disorder. They took their own life because life is not worth living to them,” Barnes says. Sometimes people suffering from certain mental illnesses need someone to talk to and confide in, “and that’s all it takes.”

Looking forward

The church plays a vital role in the culture of African Americans, with nearly 8 in 10 people identifying as Christians.

About 20 years ago, a parishioner at a local church, Healing Center in Tennessee, who had previously sought counseling for an unstated mental illness, died from a self-inflicted gunshot wound at the church. While the act left parishioners traumatized, William Young, an African American priest, then a chaplain at another local church, said the incident exposed why the church must drive mental health awareness and suicide prevention in Black communities.

Bishop Young and his wife, Dianne. Image: Daily Memphian

Bishop Young’s persistence led to the inaugural National Suicide and the Black Church Conference -- an event that addresses the root causes behind hopelessness that can lead to suicide, particularly in the African American community -- hosted by the Healing Center and is held every other year. “There was a myth that Black people didn’t commit suicide, only white people,” Bishop Young said. “The myth also says we're stronger because we had come through slavery, but the reality was that many people had died from suicide because suicide is the result of extreme depression.”

For Dr. Lashonda Green, who currently consults as a clinical psychologist in New Jersey, faith-based groups could be instrumental in promoting mental health awareness and suicide prevention among African Americans. She said that training critical people in the church on counseling is a good starting point. “I don't think they've done a good job training their people. When you're feeling down and discouraged, it's one thing to have to speak to a deacon or a church elder,” she said. “But when dealing with mass shootings, divorce, and gender issues, you need to seek help outside the church and speak with somebody who is more objective and professionally trained. I don't think the church has done enough to encourage its people to do that.”

When Jasmine didn’t get her father’s approval to seek professional therapy, she turned to Rex Agyemang, 32, her youth leader in the church. Although not a clinical psychologist or a trained counselor, Rex had some psychology knowledge from his undergraduate courses. As a leader in the youth department of Jasmine’s church, his responsibilities include providing a form of spiritual counseling in line with Christian principles. Despite performing in this role for more than five years, he believes people like him who interface with the youth need training in guidance counseling, among others. “One of the reasons I can assist people like Jasmine is because of traits that I have, my knack for wanting to help people,” said Rex. “But as far I’m concerned, sharpening this skillset through guidance counseling training would be useful in helping more people.”

For faith-based institutions like churches to help resolve the Black youth mental crisis the country faces, they must learn to communicate in the language that the youth can relate to – not just a top-bottom approach, said Olusola Adegbite, a Ph.D. candidate at the Union Theological Seminary in New York City.

degbite, a youth leader at a local church she asked not to be named because she is not an authorized spokesperson, argued that churches that do not factor in the needs of the youth in their activities are bound to achieve less meaningful results. “We think we know what we should be doing for the people, and we just like expect them to show up and participate because we are doing a lot of work making happen for them,” Adegbite said. “It's supposed to be the other way around. We need to listen to the people first and determine their greatest needs. We need to find out some ways that they are already handling whatever issues they have and find ways to enhance it.”

Adegbite also added that the manner of education needed for church members to openly acknowledge the realness of issues surrounding mental and suicide must be openly led from the pulpit. “Talking about it [mental health and related issues] from the pulpit is an important aspect of validating people's experiences because other things that we don't talk about in church, people don't feel comfortable to talk about them either,” said Adegbite.

Lydia Torgbor, who has been with the Presbyterian Church in Harlem since it was established in the mid-80s, argues that many faith-based institutions can do to demystify mental health and suicide prevention.

Torgbor, who racked three decades of experience teaching kids in public schools, was in charge of the church’s youth wing throughout her career, providing guidance counseling to hundreds of kids. She provided a listening ear and acted as a confidante for kids through her teaching. “Our church, like any other, provides that spiritual guidance to our members. We have not necessarily been deliberate about mental health awareness and suicide prevention,” said Torgbor. “But I concede the church can do a much better job in creating awareness and letting its members know that it is alright to seek care elsewhere when for instance, you are severely depressed. ”One of the church kids Torgbor raised is Rex, Jasmine’s youth leader.

The retired teacher states that given the conversation about the growing mental crisis surrounding Black youth, she would discuss the issue with the church’s leadership, discussing what mitigating role the church can play for its youth. “One of the avenues we would explore is training these youth leaders to recognize some of these common mental issues that these kids face,” Torgbor said. “These leaders spend a lot of time with these kids, and giving them that training would help immensely to help identify distressed kids.”

Stigma in the home

Jasmine’s parents are instrumental in a community where mental health awareness and suicide prevention are not openly discussed as experts say they should be. Aside from the stigma in the church, there could be other reasons for not giving in to their daughter’s request for therapy.

There’s a misconception among African American parents that something goes wrong with their kids reflects poorly on them, said Dr. Barnes. “They (parents) hide and don't want to talk about it,” she said. “They want to think that if they avoid the situation, it will go away; instead, if you avoid the problem, it worsens. While parents and families mean well, they can often hinder the tender issues of mental health and suicide.”

Robert, Jasmine’s father, recounts vividly when her daughter approached him seeking to talk to a therapist. He sees no reason why her daughter should not be able to open up to him or any family member as he had suggested. “As a parent, I felt it was right to know at least what she’s going through. At least knowing that would help me guide her on what to do,” Robert said. “After all, if the situation is beyond me, we can always seek help externally. But I still believe as her father; she should be able to open up to me first.” In Robert’s vague recollection of events, Jasmine’s mother supposedly took her to a therapist. Indeed, Jasmine never saw any therapist apart from seeking guidance from one of the youth leaders in the church, and her dad conceded that he never followed up on the issue again.

But Kim Kaiser, the Director of Diversity Equity and Inclusion at Families Together in New York State, a non-profit that advocates mental health needs of children, thinks that stigma exists among African American families and churches because people are trained to focus on their strengths and suppress their weaknesses.

“Suicide is rising exponentially amongst our Black youth, and we know we have got to work first on the stigma,” Kaiser said. “It's like decolonizing mental health therapy in your head to understand that this is bigger than just a fractured system. It is about getting your child the help that they need.” Breaking the cycle of shame among African Americans requires an extraordinary effort, but Kaiser believes that family, as the basic unit of society, has an integral role to play.

For Jasmine, who would soon be heading off to college, her parents' love is undoubted, but she only wished they would understand her emotional needs a little more.
